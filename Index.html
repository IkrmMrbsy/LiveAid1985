introducing live Aid 1985


Live Aid was a colossal rock music event held on July 13, 1985. Bob Geldof and Midge Ure organized the event to raise funds for famine relief in Ethiopia. Concerts were held simultaneously at Wembley Stadium in London (attended by about 72,000 people) and JFK Stadium in Philadelphia (attended by about 90,000 people). In addition, additional concerts were held in cities such as Sydney and Moscow. The Live Aid concert went down in history as the largest live broadcast in television history, with an estimated 1.9 billion viewers in 150 countries watching the event live on television.

The Live Aid show plan was born following Band Aid's success with the single "Do They Know It's Christmas?". The Live Aid show was organized as a large-scale concert in two cities separated by the Atlantic Ocean, London and Philadelphia. The success of this charity concert exceeded the expectations of the organizers at that time. In a 2001 television program, Harvey Goldsmith, the promoter of the Live Aid show, admitted that the concert was originally only expected to raise 1 million pounds (US$1.89 million),[1] but eventually raised 150 million pounds (US$283.6 million). Bob Geldof was knighted by the British Empire, in part for organizing the Live Aid concert.

Concert At Wembley Stadium

The concert was opened by Status Quo with "Rockin' All Over the World". The Queen group who performed "Bohemian Rhapsody" was greeted by the audience, and continued by the masses singing and clapping together with Freddie Mercury in the song "Radio Ga Ga". The crowd eagerly sang verse after verse of "We Will Rock You" and "We Are the Champions" until the performance seemed to be controlled by the crowd who took over the role of vocalist Freddie Mercury. According to recent polls, Queen's performance is considered the best part of the Live Aid concert."

Concert recording

Bob Geldof, when inviting artists to the Live Aid concert, promised that their performances would be televised only, without being recorded. ABC Television footage of the concert was deleted at Geldof's request, and a copy donated to the Smithsonian Institution is presumed lost. MTV kept some footage, but commercial cuts and host commentary detracted from the completeness of the footage. BBC television also did not delete footage, but does not have some footage from the Philadelphia performance as it never aired it.

best artist performance

U2 was one of the iconic performers at the Live Aid concert in 1985. They performed "Sunday Bloody Sunday" and "Bad," with a memorable performance. To top it off, Bono jumped into the audience to help a drowning woman, creating an iconic moment in concert history. U2's performance at Live Aid helped popularize the group globally and voiced a humanitarian message about the famine in Ethiopia.
Queen at the 1985 Live Aid concert put on an incredible performance at Wembley stadium, London. Freddie Mercury's energetic performance and interaction with the audience during the song "Radio Ga Ga" became an iconic moment in live music history. Queen helped raise funds for humanitarian causes and delivered a message of peace. Their performance is considered one of the greatest in concert history.
David Bowie's performance at Live Aid 1985 was a historic moment. In a short period of time, Bowie performed several songs, including "Heroes," and showed off his musical skills. This iconic performance helped lift the spirits of the Live Aid concert and is well remembered in music history.
Phil Collins' performance at the 1985 Live Aid concert was one of the defining moments in music history. Collins performed at two locations, in London and Philadelphia, in one day. His performance included the songs "In the Air Tonight" and "Against All Odds." Her presence at two different locations in one day was a remarkable achievement and one of the highlights of the event.
Sting's performance at the 1985 Live Aid concert was mesmerizing. He performed the songs "Roxanne" and "Message in a Bottle" with other members of The Police. His energetic performance and distinctive voice left a strong impression in the history of Live Aid concerts. Sting and The Police were among the leading performers at the event which successfully raised funds for Ethiopian humanitarian aid.
Paul McCartney's performance at the Live Aid concert in 1985 was a historic moment. He performed at Wembley stadium and performed several songs including "Let It Be." McCartney's performance united the audience with a message of peace and humanity. This performance reminds us of the huge impact music has had on charity events throughout history.
